Marie Skłodowska Curie

The first woman who won a Nobel Prize

At First Solvay Conference (1911), Curie (seated, second from right) confers with Henri Poincaré; standing, fourth from right, is Rutherford; second from right, Einstein; far right, Paul Langevin.

Marie Skłodowska Curie was:

  • a Polish and naturalized-French physicist and chemist who conducted pioneering research on radioactivity;
  • the first woman to win a Nobel Prize;
  • the first person and only woman to win twice;
  • the only person to win a Nobel Prize in two different sciences;
  • part of the Curie family legacy of five Nobel Prizes;
  • the first woman to become a professor at the University of Paris;
  • the first woman to be entombed on her own merits in the Panthéon in Paris.

Time line of Marie Skłodowska Curie's life:

  • 1867 – born in Warsaw, Kingdom of Poland
  • 1883 – graduates gymnasium for girls with a gold medal
  • 1884 – 1890 – works as governess and continues to educate herself, reading books, exchanging letters and being tutored herself
  • 1890 – 1891 – begins practical scientific training in a chemical laboratory at the Museum of Industry and Agriculture in Warsaw
  • 1891 – leaves Poland for France and enrolls at the University of Paris
  • 1893 – gets a degree in physics and begins work in an industrial laboratory of Professor Gabriel Lippmann
  • 1894 – was denied a place at Kraków University because she was a woman
  • 1895 – marries Pierre Curie
  • 1897 – her daughter Irène was born
  • 1898 – Curie and her husband publish a joint paper announcing the existence of an element which they named "polonium", in honour of her native Poland
  • 1898 – the Curies announced the existence of a second element, which they named "radium", from the Latin word for "ray"
  • 1898 – 1902 – the Curies published, jointly or separately, a total of 32 scientific papers, including one that announced that, when exposed to radium, diseased, tumor-forming cells were destroyed faster than healthy cells
  • 1903 – supervised by Gabriel Lippmann, Curie gets her doctorate from the University of Paris
  • 1903 – is awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics together with Pierre Curie and Henri Becquerel "in recognition of the extraordinary services they have rendered by their joint researches on the radiation phenomena discovered by Professor Henri Becquerel"
  • 1910 – isolates pure radium metal
  • 1911 – is awarded the Nobel Prize in Chemistry "in recognition of her services to the advancement of chemistry by the discovery of the elements radium and polonium, by the isolation of radium and the study of the nature and compounds of this remarkable element"
  • 1914 – 1918 (World War I) – sees a need for field radiological centres near the front lines to assist battlefield surgeons; procures X-ray equipment, vehicles, auxiliary generators, and developes mobile radiography units; becomes the director of the Red Cross Radiology Service and sets up France's first military radiology centre; produces hollow needles containing "radium emanation", a colorless, radioactive gas given off by radium, later identified as radon, to be used for sterilizing infected tissue
  • 1920 – for the 25th anniversary of the discovery of radium, the French government establishes a stipend for her
  • 1922 – becomes a member of the newly created International Commission for Intellectual Cooperation of the League of Nations
  • 1934 – dies at the Sancellemoz sanatorium in Passy, Haute-Savoie at age of 66 from aplastic anemia caused from long-term exposure to radiation
